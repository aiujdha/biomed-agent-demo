# Antibody-Drug Conjugates in Oncology: A Review

## Abstract

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) are a class of targeted cancer therapeutics that combine a monoclonal antibody with a cytotoxic payload via a chemical linker. This review summarizes recent advances in ADC design, clinical outcomes, and emerging resistance mechanisms.

## Introduction

ADCs leverage the specificity of monoclonal antibodies to deliver cytotoxic drugs directly to tumor cells, thereby minimizing systemic toxicity. The three components of an ADC — the antibody, the linker, and the payload — each play a critical role in determining efficacy and safety. First-generation ADCs faced challenges with linker stability and payload potency, but next-generation designs have substantially improved therapeutic indices.

## Key Clinical Results

Trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1) was the first ADC approved for solid tumors, indicated for HER2-positive metastatic breast cancer. In the EMILIA trial, T-DM1 demonstrated a median progression-free survival (PFS) of 9.6 months compared to 6.4 months with lapatinib plus capecitabine. The overall response rate was 43.6% versus 30.8%.

Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd, DS-8201) represents a significant advancement with a drug-to-antibody ratio of approximately 8:1. In the DESTINY-Breast01 trial, T-DXd showed an objective response rate of 60.9% and median PFS of 16.4 months in patients with HER2-positive metastatic breast cancer who had progressed on T-DM1.

Enfortumab vedotin, targeting Nectin-4, was approved for locally advanced or metastatic urothelial carcinoma. The EV-301 trial confirmed a median overall survival benefit of 12.9 months versus 9.0 months with standard chemotherapy.

## Resistance Mechanisms

Primary and acquired resistance to ADCs can arise through several mechanisms: (1) reduced antigen expression or antigen mutations that impair antibody binding; (2) alterations in intracellular trafficking that reduce payload release; (3) upregulation of drug efflux pumps such as MDR1; and (4) anti-apoptotic signaling that counteracts payload cytotoxicity.

## Future Directions

Next-generation ADCs are exploring bispecific antibodies, non-internalizing antibodies for tumor microenvironment targeting, and novel payloads with different mechanisms of action. Combination strategies with immune checkpoint inhibitors are also under active clinical investigation.

## Conclusion

ADCs have established themselves as an important therapeutic modality in oncology. Continued optimization of each component and rational combination strategies are expected to further expand their clinical utility.
